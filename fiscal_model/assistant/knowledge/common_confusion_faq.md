# Common public-finance confusions

This file exists to give the assistant ready answers to the
terminology traps that confuse most readers. Cite this file when
the user's question hinges on a definitional confusion.

## Deficit vs. debt

- **Deficit** = annual flow. Outlays minus revenues in a single year.
- **Debt** = stock. Cumulative deficits accumulated, minus repayments.
- A *smaller deficit* still grows debt (the deficit is positive).
- The deficit must reach **zero or surplus** for debt to fall in
  nominal terms; debt-to-GDP can fall with a small deficit if GDP
  grows fast enough.

## Debt held by the public vs. gross federal debt

- **Debt held by the public**: ~\$28 trillion (FY2024). Bonds in
  private, foreign, and state-government hands. This is the operative
  measure for fiscal sustainability and r-g analysis.
- **Gross federal debt**: ~\$34 trillion. Adds **intragovernmental
  holdings** — bonds the Social Security and other federal trust funds
  hold against the general fund. These are accounting entries within
  the federal government, not market liabilities.
- The "debt ceiling" (statutory limit) applies to **gross** debt.
- For CBO-style sustainability analysis, **always use debt held by
  the public**.

## Primary deficit vs. total deficit

- **Total deficit** = outlays − revenues (includes net interest).
- **Primary deficit** = total deficit − net interest.
- The primary deficit is the **policy-controllable** part; net
  interest reflects past debt and current rates. For the question "is
  this fiscal policy sustainable", the primary balance matters more
  than the total.

## Social Security "trust fund depletion" ≠ bankruptcy

A frequent and politically charged confusion:

- **Depletion** (currently projected 2033 for OASI): trust fund
  reserves reach zero.
- **Continuing income**: payroll tax continues to flow in, covering
  about **77% of scheduled benefits** indefinitely.
- The legal default is automatic benefit cuts to match incoming
  revenue, **not** zero payments.
- Congress can — and historically always has — restored solvency
  before this trigger fires.

## Static vs. dynamic scoring

- **Static (conventional)** = mechanical revenue effect + behavioral
  offset (taxpayers respond to rates), but holds GDP/employment/prices
  fixed. CBO's default and the app's default.
- **Dynamic** = adds macro feedback: GDP responds to fiscal stance,
  generating revenue feedback. CBO uses dynamic scoring only for
  legislation expected to have material macro impact (e.g., TCJA).
- "Dynamic scoring" is **not** automatically pro-tax-cut. Tax-cut
  dynamic effects are typically modest (5–15% offset of static cost);
  for deficit-financed cuts, the long-run effect can *raise* the cost
  via interest-rate feedback.

## Current law vs. current policy baseline

- **Current law**: scheduled events happen on schedule. TCJA expires
  end of 2025. The TCJA-extension cost is roughly **\$4.6 trillion**
  vs. this baseline.
- **Current policy**: assumes expiring provisions are extended (the
  political default). The TCJA-extension cost is roughly **\$0** vs.
  this baseline.
- CBO's official baseline is **current law**. Treasury sometimes
  reports against **current policy**.

## Marginal vs. effective tax rate

- **Marginal rate** = rate on the next dollar of income. The "top
  marginal rate" is 37% for individuals in 2024.
- **Effective rate** = total tax / total income. The top 1%'s
  effective federal individual income rate runs about 26%.
- The "37% rate" doesn't mean the top earner pays 37% on all income —
  it applies only to income above the top bracket threshold.

## Tax expenditure vs. spending

- **Tax expenditure** = revenue forgone from a preference (deduction,
  exclusion, credit, preferential rate).
- A tax expenditure can be **economically equivalent** to a spending
  program — but Congress accounts for the two differently. The
  employer-health-insurance exclusion is a \$2T tax expenditure over
  5 years, larger than nearly any direct spending program.

## Why this file exists

Definitional confusion is the #1 source of bad public-finance
arguments. Catching it early lets the rest of the answer be useful.
